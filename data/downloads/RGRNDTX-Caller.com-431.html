Reunification: How separated immigrant children are reunited with their parents

Beatriz Alvarado, Corpus Christi Caller Times Published 4:15 p.m. CT June 19, 2018 | Updated 4:58 p.m. CT June 20, 2018
CLOSE

Once in the custody of Customs and Border Protection, adults who will be prosecuted for the misdemeanor charge of illegal entry into the U.S. are handed a flier that details the "next steps for families." Beatriz Alvarado/Caller-Times

Two young girls watch a World Cup soccer match on a television from their holding area at the detention center.(Photo: Ross D. Franklin\AP)

CONNECTTWEETLINKEDINCOMMENTEMAILMORE

More adults who enter the U.S. illegally, including those who surrender themselves to immigration authorities seeking asylum, are now being prosecuted for the misdemeanor offense through what is known as the new "zero tolerance" policy. 

Adults prosecuted for entering the country are separated from their children because children cannot be jailed with their parents.

Once in the custody of Customs and Border Protection, adults are handed a flier that details the "next steps for families." 

Once in the custody of Customs and Border Protection, adults are being handed a flyer that details the "next steps for families." (Photo: Screenshot/Department of Homeland Security)

It's unclear how many detained immigrants have used the hotline listed on the flier to seek information about the whereabouts of their children. Officials with Customs and Border Protection have not released further details such as statistics related to the hotline.

It's also unclear how long children typically remain separated from their parents, said Rio Grande Valley Border patrol sector Chief Manuel Padilla. 

"There's not a set standard of how long a child will remain (under the custody) of Health and Human Services," Padilla said Sunday during a news conference at the Customs and Border Protection processing center in McAllen. 

.@CBPSouthTexas’ Manuel Padilla can not specify how long the temporary separation of families can last. pic.twitter.com/I4zIKgHPAT

— Beatriz Alvarado (@CallerBetty) June 17, 2018

Here's a breakdown of how families can be reunified, according to the U.S. Department of Homeland Security: 

Step 1

  • The adult is charged with the crime of illegal entry into the United States.

Step 2 

  • Within the next 48 hours, the adult is transferred to the custody of the Department of Justice and will be presented before a judge for having violated this law.
  • The adult's child(ren) are now under the custody of the Department of Health and Human Services, Office of Refugee Resettlement. The child(ren) will be held in a temporary child shelter or hosted by a foster family.
  • At this point, the reunification process falls on the lap of the Department of Homeland Security and the Department of Health and Human Services. 

Step 3 

  • Adults can attempt to locate their children in thee ways, according to the flyer. 
  • They can call the Immigration and Customs Enforcement call center from inside or outside a detention facility from Monday through Friday from 8 a.m. to 8 p.m.
  • They can call the ORR Parent Hotline from inside or outside a detention facility, which is available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. 
  • Friends, family, ORR staff, and legal representatives can assist the adult by emailing Immigration and Customs Enforcement. 

Step 4 

  • After the adult's court hearing, they are transferred to the custody of Immigration and Customs Enforcement. 
  • While in ICE custody, agents will work with ORR to schedule regular communication — via telephone and/or video teleconferencing — with their child(ren).
  • ICE also provides access to legal self-help materials.

This is a developing story. Check back for updates.
