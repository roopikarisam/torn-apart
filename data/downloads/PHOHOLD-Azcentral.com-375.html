    Historical Downtown Phoenix warehouse

    Crown Lace & Notion warehouse, 1970s.
    Douglas Towne, Special for The Republic Published 6:00 a.m. MT May 31, 2018

    The Western Wholesale Co. Warehouse at 101 E. Jackson St. in Phoenix was built in 1925. It housed Alice Cooper'stown, which closed in October 2017.(Photo: Michelle Dodds/Special for The Republic)

    CONNECTTWEETLINKEDINCOMMENTEMAILMORE

    After many years of enjoying a rock 'n' roll lifestyle, the Downtown Phoenix warehouse that was home to Alice Cooper’stown Restaurant has found a healthier future.

    The restaurant that featured the “Big Unit,” a 22-inch hot dog named after former Diamondbacks pitcher Randy Johnson, closed in October 2017. Equality Health, a healthcare company that focuses on helping underserved communities, has moved into the historic building at 101 E. Jackson St.

    This transition is just the latest of many tenant changes in the building that was originally built in 1925 as the Western Wholesale Drug Company warehouse.

    Warehouse's health-industry roots

    The warehouse was the regional distribution center for drugs, medicines and liquors for the California-based company, which started in Los Angeles in 1901.

    Western Wholesale Drug entered the Phoenix market in 1920 when they acquired the H.H. McNeil Printing Company warehouse. The drug company purchased the adjacent parcel the same year, and in 1925, built an adjoining warehouse. Contractor T.B. Stewart constructed the reinforced concrete and red brick building for $30,000. 

    The one-story warehouse with a full basement tripled the company’s storage capacity and allowed boxcar freight deliveries directly into the building. The warehouse’s foundation was constructed to allow for the addition of two additional floors to the structure, according to research done by Ryden Architects.

    SEE ALSO: The story of one of Phoenix Country Club's grandest homes

    The drug company joined the national McKesson & Robbins firm and emerged as McKesson-Western Wholesale Company in 1929. Business was lucrative since drug companies had been allowed to manufacture distilled liquor for medicinal purposes during Prohibition.

    Liquor and lace

    Crown Lace &amp; Notion warehouse, 1970s. (Photo: Klanco Inc.)

    After Prohibition was repealed in 1933, the firm reverted to McKesson & Robbins and became a major distributor of liquor, including the sole source for Galliano, the Italian liquor.

    McKesson & Robbins moved from the warehouse to a new location in 1961. The Crown Lace and Notion Company, which distributed sewing supplies, purchased the warehouse.

    Saul Abromovitz had founded the company in 1946, when lace was in high demand after World War II. Abromovitz purchased lace from large retail stores and repackaged it in smaller lengths to supply small businesses outside the Valley. Abromovitz and his wife, Esther, initially operated the business out their house, using their piano as a packing table.

    Crown Lace and Notion expanded into other products, including crafts, after their son, Gary, joined the business in 1971. The company eventually out grew the old Jackson Street warehouse and moved into a larger warehouse in 1975.

    The Abromovitz family kept the old historic warehouse and tried to lease it out. But it was a tough time to find tenants for Downtown buildings, according to Mark Abromovitz, the grandson of Saul Abromovitz and current manager of Klanco, Inc., which owns the property. “The tenants we did get into the building didn’t always pay,” he says with a laugh.

    One proposal for the building was Horny’s Court, a restaurant and bar in which former Phoenix Sun Jeff Hornacek was involved, but it never came to be around 1993.

    The building’s most recent tenant was Alice Cooper’stown, which opened in 1998 as one of the first nightspots in Downtown Phoenix’s warehouse district. Alice Cooper, Hall of Fame rock star and Phoenix resident, was a partner in the restaurant.

    In cities and towns across metro Phoenix, historically significant buildings face the prospect of meeting their end with the swing of a wrecking ball. Here are some of those buildings.  The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Tempe - La Casa Vieja: Construction of "The Old House," on the west side of Mill Avenue south of Rio Salado Drive, was finished in 1873. It's considered the oldest standing building in the Valley and was continually occupied until it's last occupant, Monti's La Cas Vieja restaurant, closed in 2014. Why is it in trouble? The building was purchased by a developer that's planning a high-rise office and hotel around it. The developer intends to preserve it as part of the project.  Dave Seibert / The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Tempe - Gonzalez-Martinez House: The Gonzalez-Martinez House, built in 1888, is the oldest remaining private home in Tempe. Why is it in trouble? The house is on a plot of land near First Street and Farmer Avenue. It's in a legal dispute involving the state, the city and Steve Sussex, who has claimed squatter's right because his family's lived on the property since it was built.  Charlie Leight / The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Mesa - Buckhorn Baths Motel: The Buckhorn Baths Motel was built in 1939. Its history is closely entwined with Major League Baseball -- some say the Cactus League may never have started if the owner of the New York Giants hasn't stayed at the spa. The motel at the northwest corner of Main Street and Recker Road sprung up after Ted and Alice Sliger tapped a warm, mineral-rich aquifer while drilling for cool well water. The soon operated a bustling compound that attracted tourists from around the world who wanted to take dips in the hot baths. The 15-acre-plus property has deteriorated for more than a decade.  Mesa Preservation Foundation
    Fullscreen
    Mesa - Downtown storefronts: The group of storefronts along Main Street between Robson and Center Street represents the largest concentration of historic commercial structures in Mesa. In the 1980s, the city covered several of the storefronts, originally built in the early 1900s, to add colonnades to provide shade and unify the look of Main Street. But, the renovations mean the shops no longer qualify for formal historic designations. Mayor John Giles is in favor of removing the colonnades to reveal the original architecture, but the construction could cost up to $450,000.  Michael Schennum / The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Goodyear- Phoenix Trotting Park: The Phoenix Trotting park was built near the interchange of Loop 303 and Interstate 10 in the early 1960s by James Dunnigan, a horse-racing enthusiast who also owned a track in New York. It was opened for two seasons before it was abandoned in 1966. The 194 acres that encompasses the park went up for sale in late 2015 and is currently under contract, according to John Finnegan of Colliers International. He said he does not know what the potential buyer plans to do with the property.  Tom Tingle / The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Scottsdale - Former LDS church: Originally built by the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in 1958, the building at the southwestern corner of Earll Drive and Civic Center is one of the oldest churches in Scottsdale. The city reviewed six old church buildings for historic protection a few years ago but decided the former Mormon chapel did not qualify. It was slated for demolition in 2012 to clear space for a rehabilitation center, but the plans never materialized.  Parker Leavitt / The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Scottsdale - Nuss Building: The two-story building at Scottsdale Road and Shoeman Lane in downtown Scottsdale was built in 1960. Today, the building houses a variety of small businesses, but it's also partially vacant. The building was recently purchased by Stockdale Capital Partners, which has filed plans to replace the building with a seven-story office tower. The project is working its way thru a city-approval process.  Parker Leavitt / The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Scottsdale - House of Rice strip mall: The shopping center at 3215 N. Hayden Road was built in the 1960s. Preservationist Dan Semenchuk said it's a wonderful example of midcentury modern architecture. The property is not protected under Scottsdale's historic-preservation ordinance and could be sold for redevelopment.  Parker Leavitt / The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Chandler - Downtown historic square: The rows of buildings on the west side of Arizona Avenue, between Boston and Buffalo streets, was built from 1912 to 1920. Many of them are the original structures built around Chandler's founding. Chandler currently does not have a historic-preservation ordinance that could legally protect them. The city has, however, designated the area as a City Center District, which has separate regulations for land use and development. The regulations are designed to maintain the historic identity of the area.  Chris Coppola / The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Glendale - Old Beet Sugar Factory: The iconic red-brick building, built in 1906, opened as a beet-sugar factory six years before Arizona became a state. Since then, the building has had a variety of purposes. In 1986 the building closed for good. Recent plans to restore the building have not come to fruition and the landmark continues to languish.  The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Phoenix - Mrs. Neal House: The house with a pitched roof near Margaret T. Hance Park was designed by notable architecture firm Lescher and Mahoney. It was built in 1921. The building has sat empty since Way Cool Hair closed in 2009.  The Republic
    Fullscreen
    Phoenix - WPA Administration Building: The Art Deco Works Progress Administration building on the Arizona State Fairgrounds was built in 1938 as part of The New Deal. Historic preservationists have fought to save the building since 2014, when fair officials announced they wanted to raze the dilapidated structure to make room for more vendors.  The Republic
    Fullscreen

      Equality Health, which already had a Downtown office at Third and Lincoln streets, leased the former restaurant to relocate employees from a Scottsdale location. The health company hired the architecture firm Gould Evans for the warehouse’s office conversion. Martin Building Company is the contractor for the project.

      The Equality Health offices will continue to highlight the warehouse’s historic character, including the red brick and pitched wooden trusses.  “I just love that they’re like giant boxes that you can retrofit for so many uses,” Mark Abromovitz says. “These cool old buildings are timeless, and it's great to see the tech world embracing this sort of architecture. I’m thrilled to have Equality Health as our tenant and look forward to a great partnership for many years to come."

      Douglas C. Towne is the editor of Arizona Contractor & Community magazine, www.arizcc.com.
